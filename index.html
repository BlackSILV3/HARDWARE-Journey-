STAGE 02
Semiconductor Physics
The physics inside every transistor, diode, and MOSFET. P-N junctions, carrier drift and diffusion, depletion regions, breakdown — the invisible reality that makes chips possible.
► Understand the device before you use it

Probability Note: Pierret will introduce statistical concepts around device variability, carrier distributions, and doping. Pay attention to these — probability and random variables will appear again in Stage 9 (signal integrity), Stage 10 (noise), and Stage 13 (memory reliability). No separate stage needed but take the statistics seriously here.

STAGE 03
Signals & Systems
The mathematical framework for understanding how any signal behaves in any system. Fourier, Laplace, convolution, frequency response, filtering — used in every future stage.
► Mathematical Core

Probability & Statistics Supplement: Alongside this stage, supplement with basic probability — random variables, distributions, expectation, variance, and basic stochastic processes. This feeds directly into noise analysis in Stage 10, bit-error rates in Stage 9, yield analysis in Stage 11B, and memory reliability in Stage 13. Khan Academy Statistics or MIT 6.041 (Probabilistic Systems) covers this adequately.

STAGE 04
Microelectronics & Analog Circuits
Transistor circuits — amplifiers, op-amps, biasing, frequency response. The bridge between device physics and analog IC design. Builds analog intuition that must be maintained.
► Build Analog Intuition Here — Maintain It Later

⚠ Analog Maintenance Warning: There is a significant gap between this stage and Stage 10 (Analog IC Design). Analog intuition decays without use — unlike digital, it is heavily experience-based. Between Stages 6 and 9, do at least one small analog project per stage: a small-signal amplifier, an active filter, an op-amp circuit. Revisit key Sedra & Smith chapters before starting Stage 10. This is the most important maintenance discipline in the entire roadmap.

STAGE 05B ✦ NEW
Data Structures & Algorithms
Arrays, linked lists, trees, hash tables, graphs, sorting, searching, complexity analysis. Not a full CS degree — a targeted engineering toolkit. Every EDA tool is fundamentally algorithmic.
✦ Added in v2 Review — Closes Critical Gap

Why this matters for VLSI: Synthesis tools, place-and-route engines, timing analyzers, and formal verification tools are all built on graph algorithms, dynamic programming, and tree structures. Understanding these makes you a better user of EDA tools and eventually capable of contributing to them. Embedded systems also require memory-efficient algorithms due to constrained RAM.

Embedded Linux
The bridge between bare-metal firmware and the processors you will design. Understanding how Linux boots, uses the MMU, manages caches, and talks to peripherals makes you a better processor architect.

STAGE 11B ✦ SPLIT
Physical Design, CMOS Layout & Verification
CMOS transistor-level layout, RTL-to-GDS flow, DRC/LVS, functional verification with UVM, yield analysis, and manufacturing process awareness. The full chip design back-end.
✦ Split from original Stage 11 — Second Half

Verification Gap — Closed Here: Functional verification (UVM, constrained-random testing, coverage-driven verification) is the largest part of chip design by headcount in industry. This stage explicitly addresses it. The ZipCPU blog covers formal methods. UVM is covered here for functional simulation. Both are needed.

Manufacturing Awareness: Weste & Harris covers CMOS layout and design rules. Supplement with the Skywater 130nm PDK documentation (free, from Google's OpenMPW program) to understand how the masks you draw become physical transistors. DRC and LVS are introduced here through Magic VLSI.
